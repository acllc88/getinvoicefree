What Is an Estimate (Quote)?

An estimate — also called a quote or proposal — is a document sent to a potential client before work begins. It outlines the scope of work, individual costs, and the total expected price. It's not a request for payment; it's an offer that the client can accept, negotiate, or decline.

Sending a professional estimate before starting work sets clear expectations, protects you legally, and signals to clients that you're organised and trustworthy.

Estimate vs Invoice vs Receipt

  • Estimate / Quote — Sent before work. Outlines expected costs. Not binding until accepted.
  • Invoice — Sent after work is done (or at a milestone). Requests payment.
  • Receipt — Issued after payment is received. Confirms the money was paid.

What to Include in an Estimate

  • Your business name, email, and phone
  • Client name and company
  • Estimate number and date issued
  • Validity period (e.g. "Valid for 30 days")
  • Project or subject description
  • Itemised list of services with quantities and prices
  • Tax, discounts, and total estimate amount
  • Payment terms (e.g. 50% deposit to begin)
  • Notes about what is and isn't included

Tips for Writing Winning Estimates

  • Be specific. Vague estimates lose to specific ones. List every deliverable individually.
  • Set a validity period. "Valid for 30 days" creates urgency and protects you from price changes.
  • State your payment terms upfront. "50% deposit required to begin work" filters out slow-paying clients immediately.
  • Note what's not included. "Estimate does not include stock photography or third-party software licences" prevents scope disputes.
  • Send as PDF. Never send an editable estimate — it can be altered. PDF is professional and tamper-proof.

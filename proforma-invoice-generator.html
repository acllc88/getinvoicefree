What Is a Proforma Invoice?

A proforma invoice is a preliminary bill of sale sent to a buyer before a shipment or delivery of goods. Unlike a standard invoice, a proforma is not a payment demand — it is an estimated invoice that describes the goods or services and their value, used for customs clearance, advance payment requests, or client approval before the final invoice is issued.

When Do You Need a Proforma Invoice?

  • International trade and customs declarations
  • Requesting advance or deposit payments before starting work
  • Getting client approval on pricing before issuing a final invoice
  • Insurance purposes — to declare the value of goods being shipped
  • Import/export documentation requirements
  • Letter of credit transactions

Proforma Invoice vs Standard Invoice

📋

Proforma Invoice

Sent before delivery. Not a payment demand. Used for approval, customs, deposits. Can be changed before finalising.

📄

Standard Invoice

Sent after delivery. Is a legal payment demand. Fixed and binding. Creates an accounts payable entry for the buyer.

📊

Estimate / Quote

Sent before work begins. Outlines expected costs. Not binding until accepted. Similar to proforma but used for services.

What to Include on a Proforma Invoice

  • The words "PROFORMA INVOICE" clearly at the top
  • Your business name, address, and contact details
  • Buyer name and address
  • Proforma invoice number and date
  • Description of goods or services with quantities and prices
  • Total value, currency, and applicable taxes
  • Payment terms and conditions
  • Validity period (e.g. "Valid for 30 days")

Is a proforma invoice legally binding?

No. A proforma invoice is not a legally binding document — it is a preliminary document used for informational purposes. Only a standard invoice creates a legal obligation to pay. However, once a buyer accepts a proforma and makes payment, a binding contract is typically considered to have formed.

What is the difference between a proforma invoice and a quote?

A proforma invoice looks like a real invoice and is often used for international trade and advance payments. A quote or estimate is typically used for domestic service agreements before work begins. Both are non-binding until accepted.
